Statistical bulletin:Output in the Construction Industry in Great Britain: March and Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2016

Construction output is a monthly estimate of the output of the construction industry in both the private and public sectors. The estimates are an important component of gross domestic product.

1. Main points

  • Quarterly estimate for construction output decreased by 1.1% compared with previous quarter; downward pressure came from a decrease in both new work (-0.6%) and repair and maintenance (-1.9%).

  • Quarterly estimate in GDP revised from -0.9% to -1.1%; no impact to 1 decimal place.

  • Year-on-year change also shows decrease in the quarter of 1.9%.

  • Monthly output decreased by 3.6% compared with February 2016.

  • Monthly falls again reported in new work and repair and maintenance.

2. Things you need to know

This March 2016 release contains revisions to data back to January 2016. We may see larger than average revisions to March 2016 when data are revised in future as this is the first time since the monthly series began in 2010 that Easter has fallen wholly in a March. While the seasonal pattern for a time series is generally established after 60 months in a monthly time series, there is still potential for increased revisions until the seasonal pattern has matured. For this reason users should view the quarterly series for a more well-established seasonally adjusted dataset.

The monthly business survey, Construction Output, collects output by sector from businesses in the construction industry within Great Britain. Output is defined as the amount chargeable to customers for building and civil engineering work done in the relevant period, excluding VAT and payments to subcontractors.

The survey’s results are used to produce seasonally adjusted monthly, quarterly and annual estimates of output in the construction industry at current price and at chained volume measures (removing the effect of inflation). The estimates are widely used by private and public sector institutions, particularly by the Bank of England and HM Treasury to assist in informed decision and policy making. Construction output is a key economic indicator and is therefore also used in the compilation of the output measure of gross domestic product.

On 11 December 2014 the UK Statistics Authority announced its decision to suspend the designation of Construction Output and New Orders as National Statistics due to concerns about the quality of the Construction Price and Cost Indices used to remove the effects of inflation from the statistics. We took responsibility for the publication of the Construction Price and Cost Indices from the Department of Business Innovation and Skills (BIS) on 1 April 2015, introducing an interim solution for measuring output prices in June 2015 for all periods from January 2014 onwards. We are currently developing a long-term solution for the deflation of construction statistics.

3. What does the quarterly picture show?

Figure 1: All work – quarterly time series chained volume measure, seasonally adjusted, index (2012 = 100)

Great Britain, Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2016
Source: Construction: Output and Employment – Office for National Statistics
Download this chart
Image .csv .xls

In Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2016, construction output fell by 1.1% compared with the previous quarter. There was also a fall of 1.9% compared with the same period a year ago. This is the first year-on-year decrease since Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2013.

Even though we have seen a decrease this quarter, construction output remains at a higher level than experienced during the economic downturn between 2009 and 2013.

The 2 components of construction output are all new work and repair and maintenance. In Quarter 1 all new work decreased by 0.6% compared with the previous quarter whilst repair and maintenance decreased by 1.9%. Compared with the same period a year ago, there were decreases in both components, with all new work falling by 1.3% and repair and maintenance by 3.0%.

4. What is behind the quarterly decrease?

Figure 2: Contributions to quarter-on-quarter volume growth from the main construction sectors

Great Britain, Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2016 compared with Quarter 4 (Oct to Dec) 2015
Source: Construction: Output and Employment – Office for National Statistics
Download this chart
Image .csv .xls

Figure 2 shows the contribution of each sector to output growth in the construction industry in Quarter 1 2016 compared with the previous quarter. Although the majority of work types showed a negative contribution, the largest downwards contribution came from infrastructure. Infrastructure makes up a large proportion of construction output and typically includes projects on water, electricity, railways and roads. As such, this can often be a fairly volatile series and this is now the third consecutive quarter where infrastructure has fallen.

On the other hand, new housing output continues to increase. In Quarter 1 there was an increase of 4.8% in total new housing output, with public and private new housing reporting increases of 4.2% and 4.9% respectively. This is shown in more detail in section 6.

5. Focus on: Housing this month

Figure 3: Components of all new housing, quarterly time series, seasonally adjusted, chained volume measure, £ million

Great Britain, Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2016
Source: Construction: Output and Employment – Office for National Statistics
Download this chart
Image .csv .xls

In Quarter 1 (Jan to March) 2016 there was an increase in output of 4.8% in total new housing compared with the previous quarter. Figure 3 shows that even though we saw a period of decline in growth shortly after 2007, coinciding with the economic downturn, the levels of total new housing have been steadily increasing again since 2013. The level of output is now at its highest on record at £7.5 billion.

The 2 components of new housing are public and private new housing. These sectors have also seen growth this quarter. Private new housing is by far the largest component and again the level of output for this sector is the highest on record at £6.3 billion.

6. What does the monthly picture show?

Figure 4: All work – monthly time series, chained volume measure, seasonally adjusted, index (2012 = 100)

Great Britain, March 2016
Source: Construction: Output and Employment – Office for National Statistics
Download this chart
Image .csv .xls

In March 2016 all work fell by 3.6% compared with February. This is the third consecutive month-on-month fall. Compared with March 2015 there was a fall of 1.9%, which is the first year-on-year decrease since Quarter 1 (Jan to Mar) 2013. There were also month-on-month falls in the 2 components, all new work and repair and maintenance.

Since the monthly time series began in January 2010 the monthly path has been volatile, with all new work performing at a higher level than repair and maintenance in the early and most recent part of the time series. As the monthly series is still relatively young we are likely to see more volatility until the time series has matured.

8. Links to related statistics

Output in the construction industry follows the Eurostat Short Term Statistics (STS) regulation for production in construction. Before any comparisons are made with the euro area or EU28, it is worth noting that the UK is the only member state to follow the A method for compiling production in construction statistics.

The latest release of production in construction showed that construction output in the euro area (EA19) decreased by 1.1% and by 0.4% in the EU28 in February 2016 compared with January 2016. The Great Britain estimate for February 2016 showed that construction output decreased by 1.0%. It should be noted that an accurate comparison cannot be made as Eurostat data are calculated on a 2010 = 100 basis, while Great Britain data are calculated on a 2012 = 100 basis.

Outside of the EU, the US Census Bureau release Value of construction put in place, published on 2 May 2016, showed provisional estimates of construction output increased by 0.3% in March 2016 compared with February 2016 and increased by 9.1% compared with March 2015.

9. Quality and methodology

Our monthly Construction Output survey measures output from the construction industry in Great Britain. It samples 8,000 businesses, with all businesses employing over 100 people or with an annual turnover of more than £60 million receiving a questionnaire by post every month.
